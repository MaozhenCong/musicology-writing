# Writing Quality and Style Gate

Use this reference when drafting, revising, polishing, responding to desk-rejection style criticism, or preparing dissertation chapters and journal manuscripts for final review.

This gate improves scholarly prose. It is not a detector-evasion or "humanizer" step. Never hide AI use when a journal, university, or editor requires disclosure. Discipline conventions, target-journal rules, university rules, and source evidence outrank personal style preferences.

## When to Apply

Apply this gate:

- after a claim-evidence outline exists and before heavy drafting;
- after a full draft exists and before final formatting;
- after reviewer comments mention vague theory, generic prose, over-conceptualization, or AI-like style;
- before final dissertation chapter or journal package delivery.

Do not polish prose while the argument, corpus, method, or citation base is still unstable. Fix evidence and structure first.

## Priority Order

1. Evidence and source accuracy.
2. Target journal, supervisor, or university requirements.
3. Discipline norms in musicology, music education, piano pedagogy, or reception history.
4. The user's project-specific thesis, vocabulary, and overlap boundaries.
5. Natural, modest, readable style.

Personal voice and anti-AI style rules are soft constraints. They cannot justify unsupported claims, hidden limitations, or missing citations.

## Evidence-First Paragraph Test

Before polishing a paragraph, identify:

- its function in the argument;
- the evidence it uses;
- the claim it can safely make;
- the claim it must not make;
- its relationship to the previous and next paragraph.

For dissertation chapters, also identify the chapter-level role. For reception-history work, identify the receiver group, prior horizon, evidence type, and change in meaning.

For document-based journal articles, each analytical paragraph should usually answer at least two of these questions:

- Which institution, source, event, classroom, text, repertoire, or receiver is being discussed?
- Who teaches, performs, publishes, receives, comments, learns, or institutionalizes the material?
- What exact phrase, page, archival item, or document feature supports the interpretation?
- What limited conclusion follows without claiming direct influence, classroom outcome, or audience reception?

If the paragraph cannot answer these questions, it is probably too abstract.

## Style Calibration

When the user has provided earlier drafts, accepted dissertation prose, supervisor-approved wording, or a target-journal model article, use it as a soft style profile:

- note sentence length, paragraph length, transitions, citation integration, and preferred terminology;
- preserve the user's recurring scholarly commitments and cautious tone;
- do not imitate errors, unsupported habits, or a style that conflicts with the target;
- do not import phrases from another submitted article or dissertation section when similarity risk matters.

If no style model exists, default to clear, concrete, modest academic prose.

## Anti-AI Pattern Checks

Flag and revise these patterns:

- repeated triads or formulaic lists when the evidence does not require them;
- uniform paragraph length and identical paragraph openings;
- excessive abstract nouns such as "legitimation," "mediation," "visibility," "interconnectivity," or "discourse" without nearby evidence;
- inflated verbs such as "stages," "mobilizes," "converts," "constructs," or "performs" when a simpler verb fits;
- throat-clearing openings such as "It is important to note," "This paper seeks to," or repeated "This article argues";
- "not only... but also" constructions used as default rhythm;
- synonym cycling where several near-synonyms repeat the same point;
- defensive limitation language repeated inside analysis sections.

Do not ban a term mechanically. Keep a technical term when it does analytical work and is supported by the source base.

## Musicology and Music Education Prose Rules

- Prefer source-specific sentences over broad field claims.
- Put methodological modesty mainly in the method and conclusion, not in every analysis paragraph.
- Use secondary literature to sharpen the question, method, or interpretation, not to decorate the bibliography.
- Name institutions, sources, dates, genres, pedagogical settings, media, or receiver groups when they matter.
- Avoid claiming national, institutional, pedagogical, repertoire, or student effects unless the evidence directly supports them.
- Let concepts follow evidence. Do not force every source into the same theoretical vocabulary.
- Vary transitions: contrast, sequence, evidence strength, method limit, historiographical implication, or reception shift.

## Sentence and Paragraph Revision

Revise in this order:

1. Cut unsupported generalization.
2. Replace abstract summary with the source action or claim.
3. Split mixed claims that need different citations.
4. Shorten overloaded sentences.
5. Vary rhythm by alternating compact source sentences with longer interpretive sentences.
6. Remove repeated phrase clusters across title, abstract, headings, and topic sentences.
7. Check whether the paragraph still advances the thesis after simplification.

Useful replacements:

- "makes visible" -> "shows," "presents," "records," "names," or "frames," depending on the source action;
- "institutional discourse" -> "institutional text," "public notice," "published report," "program note," or "official page" when that is what the evidence is;
- "symbolic authority" -> "the text presents the visitor as authoritative through title, affiliation, prize, role, or event format";
- "interconnectivity" -> "exchange," "circulation," "collaboration," "teaching visit," or "cross-border professional learning" when more precise.

## Desk-Rejection Risk Pass

Before finalization, run a style-focused reviewer pass that asks:

- Does the introduction state a narrow, useful contribution?
- Does the method explain what the corpus can and cannot prove?
- Are analysis sections driven by evidence rather than concept labels?
- Are key terms defined once and then used sparingly?
- Does the conclusion state a practical scholarly contribution instead of claiming too much?
- Would an editor see a human research problem, or mostly polished abstraction?

Save the style pass or include it inside the reviewer synthesis for final manuscripts, major revisions, and dissertation chapters.
