# Topic Selection and Novelty Gate

Use this reference before substantial source collection or drafting for a new article, dissertation chapter, chapter expansion, rejected-article rebuild, or concern that a topic lacks novelty, significance, or practical contribution.

## Principle

A publishable topic is not just an interesting object. It must combine:

1. a target-journal reader who has a reason to care;
2. a research problem or uncertainty, not only a theme;
3. a contribution type the article can deliver;
4. evidence that is accessible within the deadline;
5. a method matched to the evidence;
6. a practical or scholarly implication that can be stated before drafting.

If any part is missing, revise the topic before collecting a large corpus or writing prose. Do not begin with a preferred answer or theory and search only for confirming material.

## Keep the Core Terms Distinct

- **Topic:** the object or area, such as conservatory masterclasses, Chopin reception, or piano assessment.
- **Research problem:** a consequential uncertainty, disagreement, blind spot, or decision difficulty in that area.
- **Research gap:** evidence that existing scholarship cannot adequately explain, compare, document, or guide a specified part of that problem.
- **Research question:** a bounded question that the available corpus and method can answer.
- **Contribution:** the new, defensible knowledge, interpretation, tool, comparison, or boundary condition the manuscript will deliver.
- **Relevance or value:** who can use the contribution to understand, decide, teach, curate, preserve, or investigate differently.

An unstudied object is not automatically a gap. A gap exists only when the absence, limitation, disagreement, or misfit matters for a field problem and the proposed study can address it.

## Topic Development Cycle

1. State the initial object, constraints, and non-negotiables: target, language, deadline, source access, ethics, similarity boundaries, and what the project must not claim.
2. Generate at least three candidate problem-question pairs. Change the question, corpus boundary, comparison, or intended user; do not merely reword the same title.
3. Run a reconnaissance search for each viable pair: recent field work, authoritative historical work, target-journal models, cited/citing sources, and credible counterexamples.
4. Make a provisional field map: agreements, disputes, methods, corpora, omissions, and unresolved consequences. Record search dates, sources searched, and why the sample is sufficient or still incomplete.
5. Write a gap chain: `field finding -> limitation or tension -> consequence -> answerable question -> proposed contribution`.
6. Test the strongest counterargument: what would make the proposed gap ordinary, already addressed, or unimportant? Narrow, reframe, or stop if the answer defeats the claim.
7. Select one primary contribution and at most one secondary contribution. Preserve rejected options and the reason for rejection in working notes.

Two or three search rounds are usually enough for an initial decision only when later rounds mostly retrieve already-mapped work. Otherwise record the gap as provisional, not proven. A search log is evidence of due diligence, not proof of global exhaustiveness. Call a gap **evidence-backed** only when the decision record identifies the searches, coverage, field-map sources, and remaining uncertainty; never use “proven” to mean globally exhaustive.

## Research-Problem and Gap Chain

Write each element in one or two sentences and link it to actual sources:

1. **Observed field situation:** What do reliable sources show is happening or being argued?
2. **Tension or limitation:** Where do sources conflict, omit a necessary distinction, rely on an unsuitable corpus, or leave a decision unresolved?
3. **Consequence:** What cannot be interpreted, taught, documented, compared, or decided well if this remains unresolved?
4. **Question:** What can this corpus and method genuinely answer?
5. **Contribution:** What will the answer change in the scholarly conversation or a named practice?

Avoid these invalid gap claims:

- “No one has studied X” based only on a small keyword search.
- “This is the first study” without a documented, field-wide search and a defensible scope.
- “The literature is limited” without naming its corpus, method, argument, period, language, or consequence.
- “This has practical value” without a user, decision, practice, or plausible mechanism.

## Contribution Types

Choose one primary contribution type and, at most, one secondary type.

- **New corpus:** makes an underused source set available and interpretable.
- **New case:** shows how a known issue behaves in a specific institution, place, repertoire, or teaching context.
- **New comparison:** compares two cases, periods, institutions, curricula, or pedagogical systems in a way that changes interpretation.
- **New method:** applies a method that clarifies evidence that was previously anecdotal or impressionistic.
- **New practice implication:** identifies a concrete problem in pedagogy, curriculum, assessment, documentation, or institutional work and offers evidence-based guidance.
- **Boundary condition:** shows where a known claim does not fully apply.
- **Reinterpretation:** changes the meaning of known evidence by reading it through a historically or institutionally warranted problem.
- **Translation or connection:** brings separated linguistic, archival, disciplinary, or professional conversations into a comparison that changes their interpretation.

Do not claim "first," "unique," or "unprecedented" unless the literature search proves it.

## Practical-Relevance Pathway

State relevance as a bounded pathway, not a promise:

`finding -> named beneficiary or reader -> decision, interpretation, or practice that can be reconsidered -> evidence of current use or a realistic next step`

For musicology, value may be a more accurate archive description, repertoire history, curatorial interpretation, translation, public-programme framing, or historiographical correction. For music education, it may be a curriculum, assessment, teacher-learning, student-support, documentation, or institutional-design implication. These are legitimate only when the corpus supports the link.

Use **demonstrated impact** only when evidence shows an actual effect or benefit. Use **potential use**, **implication**, or **recommendation for consideration** for a proposed pathway. Do not claim improved learning, policy change, cultural participation, or institutional outcomes without evidence.

## Eight-Test Topic Gate

Score each test 0-2.

1. **Reader fit:** Would the target journal's readers recognize this as their problem?
2. **Problem strength:** Does the proposal name a consequential uncertainty rather than an attractive topic?
3. **Gap evidence:** Do mapped sources show the specific limitation, tension, or absence claimed?
4. **Question strength:** Is there a guiding question that cannot be answered by common sense and that permits a meaningful negative or qualified answer?
5. **Evidence feasibility:** Can the needed primary and secondary sources be gathered, read, and verified within the constraints?
6. **Method fit:** Does the method produce findings rather than only elegant commentary?
7. **Contribution clarity:** Can the article state what it adds without “first,” “unique,” or vague novelty?
8. **Relevance integrity:** Can it state who may use the result and how, without promising unproven impact?

Decision:

- A topic may proceed to source collection only when **reader fit**, **problem strength**, **question strength**, **evidence feasibility**, **method fit**, **contribution clarity**, and **relevance integrity** are each at least **1**, and **gap evidence = 2**. A practical-value claim with relevance integrity = 0 is invalid; either provide a bounded scholarly relevance statement or remove the claim.
- 13-16 plus every hard threshold: proceed to source collection with a stated counterargument and evidence plan.
- 9-12, or any failed hard threshold: remain `exploratory/provisional`; revise the problem, gap, corpus, contribution, or relevance before drafting.
- 0-8: stop or choose a different topic.

## Topic Decision Record

Create a short project file with:

- target journal and verified scope signals;
- current title;
- target reader;
- decision date and decision owner;
- research problem;
- research questions;
- search ledger with source, query, date, scope/language, inclusion/exclusion, and saturation result;
- field map and gap-chain ledger, including source IDs/pages, counterexamples, and remaining uncertainty;
- proposed contribution type;
- corpus and method;
- contribution sentence and the strongest plausible counterargument;
- practical-relevance pathway, labelled demonstrated impact or potential use;
- overlap risks;
- eight-test score by criterion, hard-threshold result, and decision;
- next evidence needed.

## Musicology and Music Education Calibration

For musicology journals, the problem may be historical, archival, interpretive, reception-based, or historiographical.

For music education journals, the problem should usually concern pedagogy, curriculum, teaching and learning, teacher education, assessment, policy, learner experience, or educational institutions. A music education topic that only describes repertoire, concerts, or institutional prestige is weak unless it shows why the finding matters for teaching, learning, curriculum, or educational practice.

For dissertation chapters, identify why the problem is necessary to the dissertation's overall argument and what adjacent chapter should not repeat. A chapter may develop a contribution that is cumulative rather than independently publishable, but it still needs a research problem, evidence boundary, and chapter-level stake.

For fast doctoral publication, prefer the smallest topic that passes the eight tests over a larger topic that sounds more impressive.

## Revision Moves When a Topic Feels Flat

- Change object-description into a problem: from "international masterclasses exist" to "what educational functions do official documents assign to masterclasses, how do those functions differ across institutions, and what remains undocumented about feedback or follow-up?"
- Add a comparison: institution type, event type, pre/post-pandemic period, Chinese vs partner-institution descriptions, or teacher-facing vs student-facing texts.
- Add an evaluative framework: documentation quality, stated learning outcomes, feedback structure, student role, assessment visibility, or follow-up mechanism.
- Replace vague novelty with a usable output: coding framework, typology, checklist, model, or boundary condition.
- Test a negative or qualified outcome: ask what the corpus might show that would limit a familiar story, not merely confirm it.
- Make relevance specific: replace “helps music education” with “gives curriculum designers a documented way to distinguish stated internationalisation from observable learning support.”
- Reduce the corpus if a smaller case can answer a sharper question.

## Stop Conditions

Stop and redesign if:

- the topic is only a list of events;
- the contribution is "this has not been studied" but no field problem is named;
- the evidence cannot support the claimed learning, influence, or reception;
- the proposed article would mostly repeat another submitted article's frame;
- the target journal's scope would treat the article as adjacent rather than central.
- the practical-value claim depends on outcomes the corpus cannot observe.
